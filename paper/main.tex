\begin{abstract}
DNN accelerators that run inference around the clock degrade in ways general-purpose chips usually do not. Three transistor-level mechanisms drive this: NBTI shifts threshold voltages, HCI eats away at drain current, and TDDB eventually breaks gate oxides outright. Existing reliability tools mostly look at individual circuit paths and report the present state. They cannot tell a runtime scheduler where degradation is heading next, which is the question that actually matters for operational lifetime management. We tackle this from a different angle. The accelerator is represented as a 28-node heterogeneous graph (16 MAC clusters, 8 SRAM banks, 4 NoC routers), and a Hybrid GNN--Transformer learns per-component aging from physics-grounded labels. On 40,000 simulated snapshots the predictor reaches $R^2 = 0.9976$ with a mean absolute percentage error of about $0.28\%$, and a trajectory head extends this to a 10-step forecast at $R^2 = 0.866$. Two downstream consumers exploit these predictions. NSGA-II searches the layer-to-cluster mapping space across three objectives---peak aging, latency and energy---and returns 91 Pareto-optimal mappings across five workloads, with peak-aging cuts between $49\%$ (MobileNetV2) and $70\%$ (BERT-Base). A PPO controller then takes over at runtime, learning when to rotate, balance or hold the current mapping. Compared to an unmanaged baseline and a greedy hotspot-migration policy, PPO converges to a mean episode reward of about $+1.06$. Results are based entirely on a physics-driven analytical simulator, which we discuss as the main limitation.
\end{abstract}

DNN accelerator, transistor aging, NBTI, HCI, TDDB, graph neural network, transformer, multi-objective optimization, reinforcement learning, reliability, lifetime management

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Imagine a hyperscaler running BERT-Base inference at near-saturation utilization for eighteen months. The same MAC clusters fire on every transformer block. The same SRAM banks cycle through weights and activations again and again. After roughly a year of duty, the centre of the PE array starts running noticeably hotter than its corners. Timing margins narrow. Single-event recoverable faults turn into intermittent miscompares. Eventually a few clusters fail outright. By the time monitoring catches it, the chip has been silently corrupting outputs for weeks.

This is the practical face of transistor aging in always-on inference hardware. Three mechanisms dominate. \emph{Negative Bias Temperature Instability (NBTI)} shifts threshold voltages under sustained gate bias, slowly stretching critical-path delays~\cite{hill2022cmos}. \emph{Hot Carrier Injection (HCI)} hammers drain currents in the most heavily switched transistors, biased toward the datapath~\cite{kim2023reliability}. \emph{Time-Dependent Dielectric Breakdown (TDDB)} is the loud one---once the gate oxide goes, the device is gone. The three do not act independently either. A hot cluster ages faster, gets compensated by routing more work to it through reactive load balancing, and ages faster still. The feedback loop is unkind.

Existing aging-aware design tools are not really built for this regime. Most operate at the circuit-path level. STTN-GAT~\cite{bu2024multiview}, the strongest recent example, predicts path-delay degradation with $R^2 = 0.981$, and GNN4REL~\cite{alrahis2022gnn4rel} reaches roughly $0.89$ using PNA aggregation. Earlier work like AaDaM~\cite{ebrahimipour2020aadam} sits around $R^2 \approx 0.72$ using a feed-forward network on cell-level features. All three are designed for timing signoff. Useful, but a different problem. A runtime scheduler cannot remap circuit paths; it can shuffle workloads across clusters. So the granularity gap between circuit-path estimation and architecture-level decision-making is real, and the literature has not really closed it.

There is also a second gap that, in our view, matters more. Every prior method we found reports current degradation. None of them forecasts where degradation is going. Two clusters with identical present-day aging scores can have very different trajectories---one stabilizing, one accelerating---and a runtime that cannot tell them apart will keep treating them the same. Diagnostic estimation is not the same as prognostic planning, and the latter is what actually drives proactive lifetime management.

\textbf{Contributions.} This paper does five things, ordered by what we think matters most:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item A \emph{component-level} predictor of accelerator aging built from a Hybrid GNN--Transformer. It reaches $R^2 = 0.9976$ on a 40k-snapshot dataset, which surpasses the strongest circuit-level baselines we are aware of, despite operating on a coarser and arguably harder target.

    \item A trajectory head that produces 10-step forward forecasts of per-node aging at $R^2 \approx 0.866$. To our knowledge no published method does this at the component level for DNN accelerators.

    \item An NSGA-II workload optimizer over three objectives (peak aging, latency, energy) with hypervolume-based early stopping and a SHA-1 evaluation cache. We obtain 91 Pareto-optimal mappings across five workloads, with peak-aging reductions of $49.2\%$--$69.9\%$.

    \item A PPO runtime controller that consumes both the current aging vector and the trajectory forecast. It outperforms an unmanaged baseline and a greedy hotspot-migration policy under our reward model.

    \item A reproducible end-to-end pipeline (predictor $\to$ NSGA-II $\to$ PPO $\to$ evaluation) wired together for ResNet-50, BERT-Base, MobileNetV2, EfficientNet-B4, and ViT-B/16, with full code and figures published alongside the paper.
\end{enumerate}

We are upfront about the dataset being synthetic. Section~\ref{sec:limitations} discusses what that costs us.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\textbf{Circuit-level aging prediction.} The most directly comparable line of work uses graph learning over circuit netlists. Bu et al.~\cite{bu2024multiview} report STTN-GAT, which fuses spatial--temporal transformer attention with graph attention to predict per-path delay degradation, hitting $R^2 = 0.981$ at MAPE of $3.96\%$. Alrahis et al.~\cite{alrahis2022gnn4rel} use principal neighbourhood aggregation in GNN4REL and reach $R^2 \approx 0.89$ at $8.66\%$ MAPE. Ebrahimipour et al.~\cite{ebrahimipour2020aadam} take an architectural step back with AaDaM, a feed-forward delay predictor sitting around $R^2 \approx 0.72$. All three solve circuit-path or cell-delay estimation, which is not the same task as ours. A runtime scheduler does not have a netlist in front of it; it has a list of MAC clusters and a workload to dispatch.

\textbf{Multi-objective hardware optimization.} For 2--4 objective design-space search, NSGA-II~\cite{deb2002nsga} remains the workhorse. Differential-evolution variants~\cite{das2016recent,ikushima2021de,storn1997de} have explored adaptive operator selection, sometimes with better convergence in continuous spaces. Our problem is discrete (integer chromosomes mapping layers to clusters) and modest-dimensional, which favours NSGA-II's crowding-distance mechanism. We did experiment briefly with DE, but the integer-rounding repair made it brittle in early runs, and we kept NSGA-II.

\textbf{RL for hardware management.} PPO~\cite{schulman2017ppo} has been applied to DVFS, cache management, prefetching, and thermal throttling. We could not find a published treatment of aging-aware workload migration in DNN accelerators using PPO. The combination of (i) a learned aging predictor, (ii) a learned trajectory forecast, and (iii) a PPO controller that consumes both, in a single pipeline, is what we believe is novel.

\section{System Architecture}
\label{sec:architecture}

\begin{figure}[t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{00_summary.png}
    \caption{Overview of results. Four panels summarize predictor accuracy versus literature baselines, the architecture ablation, the per-workload NSGA-II reduction in peak aging, and the PPO learning curve.}
    \label{fig:summary}
\end{figure}

The pipeline is six stages. A roofline-style analytical simulator emits per-component activity for a workload mapped onto a candidate configuration. An activity extractor converts that into the eight-feature vector per node described below. The accelerator graph itself is fixed by the configuration. The Hybrid GNN--Transformer takes the graph and features and produces both an aging score and a 10-step trajectory per node. NSGA-II uses these (plus latency and energy estimates) to produce Pareto-optimal mappings, and PPO learns to act on the resulting state at runtime. Fig.~\ref{fig:summary} shows the headline numbers.

\subsection{Accelerator graph}
\label{sec:graph}

We model the target as a heterogeneous directed graph $G = (V, E)$ with three node types:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{MAC clusters} $v_0, \ldots, v_{15}$: 16 multiply--accumulate units in a $4 \times 4$ PE array.
    \item \textbf{SRAM banks} $v_{16}, \ldots, v_{23}$: 8 banks of 256\,KB each.
    \item \textbf{NoC routers} $v_{24}, \ldots, v_{27}$: 4 routers in a fully-connected mesh.
\end{itemize}

That gives $|V| = 28$ at the default configuration. Edges are typed: compute-to-memory (weight 1.0, latency 1 cycle) connect each MAC cluster $m$ to SRAM banks $m \bmod 8$ and $(m+1) \bmod 8$; memory-to-network (weight 2.0, latency 2 cycles) connect SRAM bank $b$ to router $b \bmod 4$; and router-to-router mesh links (weight 4.0, latency 3 cycles) close the inter-cluster fabric. Edge weights and latencies appear in $\mathbf{A} \in \mathbb{R}^{|E|\times 2}$; the PyTorch Geometric \texttt{Data} object stores everything as $(\mathbf{X}, \mathbf{E}, \mathbf{A})$ with $\mathbf{X} \in \mathbb{R}^{28 \times 8}$.

\subsection{Per-node features}
\label{sec:features}

Each node carries an 8-dimensional feature vector $\mathbf{x}_i \in [0,1]^8$:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Switching activity} $x_0$: fraction of transistors switching per cycle.
    \item \textbf{Compute utilization} $x_1$: only meaningful for MACs (zero for SRAM/router).
    \item \textbf{Memory access rate} $x_2$: latency-normalized for MACs, raw access fraction for SRAM, traffic for routers.
    \item \textbf{Duty cycle} $x_3$: fraction of time the unit is active.
    \item \textbf{Temperature proxy} $x_4$: $\min(1, c_0 + c_1 u + c_2 e)$ with type-specific coefficients.
    \item \textbf{Node type} $x_5$: $\{0, 1, 2\}$ for MAC/SRAM/Router.
    \item \textbf{Workload type} $x_6$: 1 for attention-heavy (BERT, ViT), 0 otherwise.
    \item \textbf{Stress time} $x_7$: $\log(\max(t,1)) / \log(T_{\max})$ with $T_{\max} = 1.8 \times 10^6$\,s.
\end{enumerate}

The temperature proxy is just that---a proxy. It is not a thermal simulation. We discuss this in the limitations section. Activity profiles come from a roofline-style simulator that estimates throughput as $\min(\text{OPs/cycle} \cdot f, \, \text{DRAM\_BW} \cdot \text{OI})$ for each layer, where OI is operational intensity (FLOPs per byte).

\subsection{Physics-based aging labels}
\label{sec:aging_models}

We label nodes using a weighted combination of three established degradation models. None of them is novel; what we use them for is.

\subsubsection{NBTI}
A standard power law for threshold-voltage shift:
\begin{equation}
    \Delta V_{th} = A \cdot (\alpha t)^n
    \label{eq:nbti}
\end{equation}
with $\alpha$ the switching activity, $t$ the stress time in seconds, and $A = 0.005$, $n = 0.25$ at our default. For accumulation across stress windows, we recover the equivalent prior time as $t_{eq} = (\Delta V_{th,\text{prev}}/A)^{1/n}/\alpha$ and re-evaluate at $t_{eq} + \Delta t$. This is needed because real workloads run in bursts.

\subsubsection{HCI}
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\Delta I_d}{I_d} = B \cdot J^m \cdot \sqrt{t}
    \label{eq:hci}
\end{equation}
with $B = 10^{-4}$ and $m = 0.5$. The current density $J$ is taken proportional to switching activity times utilization.

\subsubsection{TDDB}
A Weibull-style failure probability:
\begin{equation}
    F(t) = 1 - \exp\!\big(-\exp(kE - \beta)\big)
    \label{eq:tddb}
\end{equation}
with $k = 2.5$ and $\beta = 10.0$, and $E$ taken proportional to switching activity times $V_{dd}$.

\subsubsection{Combined score}
\begin{equation}
    s_i = w_1 \hat{s}_{\text{NBTI},i} + w_2 \hat{s}_{\text{HCI},i} + w_3 \hat{s}_{\text{TDDB},i}, \quad s_i \in [0,1]
    \label{eq:aging_score}
\end{equation}
with normalizations $\hat{s}_{\text{NBTI}} = \min(\Delta V_{th}/0.2, 1)$, $\hat{s}_{\text{HCI}} = \min(\Delta I_d/0.1, 1)$, $\hat{s}_{\text{TDDB}} = F(t)$, and weights $w_1 = w_2 = 0.4$, $w_3 = 0.2$. The choice of weights reflects a deliberate emphasis on NBTI/HCI (slow, gradual) over TDDB (rare, catastrophic), since the controller is meant to manage the former and just avoid the latter. We did not sweep these weights exhaustively; Section~\ref{sec:limitations} comes back to this.

\subsection{Hybrid GNN--Transformer predictor}
\label{sec:model}

\begin{figure}[t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{02_ablation.png}
    \caption{Architecture ablation. Each added component---GAT attention, then transformer encoder, then prediction head---contributes a meaningful $R^2$ jump. The transformer's contribution is, to be honest, larger than we expected on a graph this small.}
    \label{fig:ablation}
\end{figure}

The model is four stages stacked. Input projection ($8 \to 256$), three GCN layers~\cite{kipf2017gcn} with residuals and batch norm, one 4-head GAT layer~\cite{velickovic2018gat} with residual, then two transformer encoder layers with 4 heads and FFN dimension $4d = 1024$. A 3-layer MLP head ($256 \to 128 \to 64 \to 1$, ReLU, 0.1 dropout) ends in a sigmoid.

\subsubsection{Why this stack?}
The GCN block extracts local topology features. GAT lets the model learn which neighbours actually matter for aging propagation; we observed empirically that a heavily loaded MAC cluster's prediction depends much more on its connected SRAM bank than on a distant router, and unweighted GCN aggregation cannot express that. The transformer is the part that surprised us. On a 28-node graph, two GCN hops already cover most of the array, so we expected the transformer to be near-redundant. It was not. Inspecting attention weights, the transformer consistently attended to nodes running the same workload type regardless of graph distance---a pattern that local message passing simply cannot represent.

The forward pass is:
\begin{align}
    \mathbf{H}^{(0)} &= \mathbf{X} \mathbf{W}_{\text{proj}} + \mathbf{b}_{\text{proj}}, \quad \mathbf{W}_{\text{proj}} \in \mathbb{R}^{8 \times 256} \\
    \mathbf{H}^{(\ell+1)} &= \mathbf{H}^{(\ell)} + \text{ReLU}\big(\text{BN}(\text{GCN}(\mathbf{H}^{(\ell)}, \mathbf{E}))\big), \; \ell = 0,1,2 \\
    \mathbf{H}_{\text{att}} &= \mathbf{H}^{(3)} + \text{BN}\big(\text{GAT}_{4}(\mathbf{H}^{(3)}, \mathbf{E})\big) \\
    \mathbf{H}_{\text{glob}} &= \text{TransformerEncoder}(\mathbf{H}_{\text{att}} + \mathbf{P}) \\
    \hat{y}_i &= \sigma\big(\text{MLP}(\mathbf{h}_{\text{glob}, i})\big)
\end{align}
where $\mathbf{P}$ is sinusoidal positional encoding.

\subsection{Trajectory head}
\label{sec:trajectory}

The trajectory predictor takes encoder features $\mathbf{h}_i$ concatenated with the current aging prediction $\hat{y}_i$:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{\mathbf{y}}_i^{\text{traj}} = \sigma\big(\text{MLP}_4([\mathbf{h}_i \,\|\, \hat{y}_i])\big) \in [0,1]^{10}.
\end{equation}
The MLP is 4 layers with dimensions $[512, 512, 256, 10]$, GELU activations, 0.1 dropout. The loss is discounted MSE plus a variance-matching term:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{\text{traj}} = \frac{1}{K} \sum_{k=1}^{K} \gamma^k (\hat{y}^{(k)} - y^{(k)})^2 + \lambda \,\big|\text{Var}(\hat{\mathbf{y}}) - \text{Var}(\mathbf{y})\big|
    \label{eq:traj_loss}
\end{equation}
with $\gamma = 0.95$, $K = 10$, $\lambda = 0.02$. The variance term is small but earns its keep---without it, predictions over-smoothed across nodes, hiding the very hotspots we wanted the controller to react to.

\subsection{NSGA-II mapping optimizer}
\label{sec:nsga}

A workload of $L$ layers maps onto $C$ clusters via integer chromosomes $\mathbf{m} \in \{0, \ldots, C-1\}^L$. We minimize three objectives:
\begin{equation}
    \min_{\mathbf{m}} \big[\,f_1(\mathbf{m}),\; f_2(\mathbf{m}),\; f_3(\mathbf{m})\,\big]
\end{equation}
where $f_1 = \max_i s_i + \beta \cdot \text{Var}(\mathbf{s})$ (peak aging plus a balance penalty, $\beta = 0.3$), $f_2$ is normalized inference latency, and $f_3$ is normalized energy. The $\beta$ term is what stops NSGA-II from finding the cheap minimum-peak solution that just dumps everything onto one or two clusters and leaves the rest cold.

The population is small ($N_{\text{pop}} = 20$) and we are aware that this is on the low end for a $16^{15}$ search space. To compensate, we initialize from four diverse seeding strategies (single-cluster, round-robin, load-balanced shuffled, perturbed RR) and rely heavily on the SHA-1 evaluation cache, which avoids re-evaluating duplicates. Genetic operators are SBX crossover ($p_c = 0.9$, $\eta = 15$) and PM mutation ($p_m = 0.1$, $\eta = 20$), with integer-rounding repair. Convergence stops when relative hypervolume improvement stays under $10^{-6}$ for 15 consecutive generations.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{NSGA-II with hypervolume convergence}
\label{alg:nsga}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Workload $W$, accelerator config $\mathcal{A}$
\Ensure Pareto-optimal set $\mathcal{P}$
\State $\text{pop} \gets$ \Call{SeedPopulation}{$N_{\text{pop}}=20$, 4 strategies}
\State $\text{cache} \gets \emptyset$, $\text{stale} \gets 0$, $\text{hv}_{\text{prev}} \gets 0$
\For{$g = 1$ to $g_{\max}$}
    \For{each chromosome $\mathbf{m}$ in pop}
        \State $h \gets \text{SHA1}(\mathbf{m})$
        \If{$h \notin \text{cache}$}
            \State Evaluate $[f_1, f_2, f_3]$ via simulator
            \State $\text{cache}[h] \gets [f_1, f_2, f_3]$
        \EndIf
    \EndFor
    \State Apply non-dominated sorting and crowding distance
    \State $\text{hv} \gets$ \Call{Hypervolume}{$\mathcal{F}$, ref$=[1, 10^9, 10^9]$}
    \If{$(\text{hv} - \text{hv}_{\text{prev}})/|\text{hv}_{\text{prev}}| < 10^{-6}$}
        \State $\text{stale} \gets \text{stale} + 1$
    \Else
        \State $\text{stale} \gets 0$
    \EndIf
    \If{$\text{stale} \geq 15$} \textbf{break}
    \EndIf
    \State SBX crossover ($p_c=0.9$, $\eta=15$) + PM mutation ($p_m=0.1$, $\eta=20$)
    \State Integer rounding repair
\EndFor
\State \Return non-dominated solutions $\mathcal{P}$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{PPO runtime controller}
\label{sec:ppo}

The runtime piece is a Gymnasium environment in which the agent observes accelerator state and picks a remapping action.

\subsubsection{State}
The observation $\mathbf{o} \in \mathbb{R}^{352}$ concatenates: current aging vector $\mathbf{s} \in \mathbb{R}^{28}$, flattened trajectory forecast $\hat{\mathbf{y}}^{\text{traj}} \in \mathbb{R}^{280}$, workload embedding $\mathbf{w} \in \mathbb{R}^{16}$, current mapping $\mathbf{m} \in \mathbb{R}^{15}$, episode progress $p \in [0,1]$, and per-node budget margin $\boldsymbol{\delta} \in \mathbb{R}^{28}$. Online normalization uses Welford's algorithm.

\subsubsection{Actions}
Five discrete options:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{0}: hold (no change)
    \item \textbf{1}: move heaviest layer from hottest cluster to coolest
    \item \textbf{2}: rotate all layers by one cluster
    \item \textbf{3}: flip one layer to opposite half of array
    \item \textbf{4}: defer to lifetime planner's recommendation
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Reward}
\begin{equation}
    r = \text{clip}\big(0.5 \cdot \Delta_{\text{aging}} + 0.3 \cdot b + 0.2 \cdot \Delta_{\text{lat}}, -2, 2\big)
    \label{eq:reward}
\end{equation}
where $\Delta_{\text{aging}} = (s_{\text{base}} - s_{\text{curr}})/s_{\text{base}}$ is relative peak-aging improvement, $b = 1 - \sigma(\mathbf{s})/\mu(\mathbf{s})$ is a balance score, and $\Delta_{\text{lat}}$ is relative latency improvement. We chose the weights $(0.5, 0.3, 0.2)$ by short pilot runs; more rigorous weight selection would benefit from a Pareto-style sweep across reward terms, which we leave as future work.

\subsubsection{Policy network}
Shared Actor--Critic trunk with two residual blocks at hidden dim 256, separate heads for action logits and value. Trunk init is orthogonal with gain $\sqrt{2}$, the actor head with gain 0.01 (so the initial policy is near-uniform and exploration is genuinely exploratory), the critic head with gain 1.0.

\subsubsection{Training}
PPO~\cite{schulman2017ppo} with clipped surrogate, 128-step rollouts, minibatch 64, 4 PPO epochs per update, $\gamma = 0.99$, GAE $\lambda = 0.95$, clip $\epsilon = 0.2$, double-clipped value loss. Learning rate decays linearly from $3\times 10^{-4}$ to $3 \times 10^{-5}$. Entropy coefficient anneals from $0.01$ to $0.001$. We early-stop a PPO update if $\text{KL} > 1.5 \times 0.03$, which avoids the destructive policy jumps that PPO is prone to in non-stationary environments---the aging dynamics shift as the agent's own actions reshape the stress distribution.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Accelerator}
$4 \times 4$ PE array, 16 MACs, 8 SRAM banks at 256\,KB each, 4 NoC routers, 1\,GHz at 0.8\,V, 51.2\,GB/s DRAM bandwidth, 512\,GB/s NoC bandwidth. This is roughly the configuration of a mid-range edge inference accelerator.

\subsection{Workloads}
Five, chosen for architectural diversity:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{ResNet-50}: 50-layer CNN, 25.6M params, mostly $3 \times 3$ convolutions, high MAC utilization.
    \item \textbf{BERT-Base}: 12-layer transformer encoder, 110M params, large QKV and FFN matrix multiplies.
    \item \textbf{MobileNetV2}: depthwise-separable CNN, lower per-cluster utilization but more uniform across the array.
    \item \textbf{EfficientNet-B4}: compound-scaled CNN, MBConv blocks of variable shape.
    \item \textbf{ViT-B/16}: Vision Transformer, 86M params, patch embedding plus 12 transformer blocks.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Dataset}
40,000 simulated accelerator snapshots for training, 5,000 each for validation and test. Each sample is a PyTorch Geometric graph with 28 nodes, 8 features per node, ground-truth aging from Eq.~\ref{eq:aging_score}, and 10-step trajectory labels. Stress times are sampled log-uniformly from $10^2$ to $10^6$\,s.

\subsection{Training}
Predictor: 100 epochs, AdamW, LR $10^{-4}$, weight decay $10^{-5}$, cosine annealing, batch 64. Trajectory: 120 epochs, monitoring validation $R^2$ for early stopping with patience 15. PPO: 5,120 timesteps, eval every 5 iterations.

\subsection{Metrics}
$R^2$, MAE, RMSE, MAPE for prediction. For NSGA-II: number of Pareto-optimal solutions, peak-aging reduction relative to the worst initial seed, and convergence generation. For PPO: episode-mean reward and best-episode reward.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Aging prediction}

The Hybrid GNN--Transformer reaches $R^2 = 0.9976$ on the 40k test set with MAE of $0.0028$ and MAPE of about $0.28\%$. Table~\ref{tab:predictor_comparison} puts this next to the prior work we could find. Two clarifications matter. First, the comparison is across granularities---STTN-GAT and GNN4REL operate on circuit paths, AaDaM on cells, ours on architectural components. We are not claiming a strict apples-to-apples improvement; we are claiming that at the accelerator-component level, where decisions actually get made, the model is well-calibrated. Second, the high $R^2$ is helped by the fact that aging in our setup is a smooth function of activity and stress time, with no hardware variability or measurement noise. Real silicon will be messier (Section~\ref{sec:limitations}).

Table~\ref{tab:ablation} progressively adds components. Adding GAT contributes the largest single increase in $R^2$, which fits intuition---attention lets the model down-weight neighbours whose state is not informative for a given target node. The transformer's contribution is the part we did not predict in advance. On a graph this small, two GCN hops cover almost all reachable structure, so we initially expected transformer self-attention to be marginal. It is not. Looking at the learned attention patterns, we suspect---we cannot prove it cleanly---that the transformer is picking up on cross-array workload-type correlations: any two MAC clusters running the same logical layer of BERT will age in correlated ways, even if they sit on opposite ends of the array. Local message passing has no good way to express that.

Fig.~\ref{fig:ablation} visualizes the same data. Improvements diminish as expected, but none of the components is dead weight.

\subsection{Trajectory forecasting}

The 10-step trajectory predictor reaches $R^2 = 0.866$ aggregated over all forecast steps, with MAE $\approx 0.040$. This is genuinely lower than the single-step accuracy, and that is fine---uncertainty compounds across a 10-step horizon and the discount factor $\gamma = 0.95$ in the loss explicitly trades distant-step accuracy for near-term precision.

Fig.~\ref{fig:traj_horizon} shows what happens when we vary the horizon length itself. At $K = 5$ aggregate $R^2$ is around $0.91$; at $K = 10$ we get our reported $0.866$; at $K = 15$ we drop to about $0.83$. The longer horizon does not help downstream control much in our setup, because the PPO controller acts on time scales much shorter than 15 forecast steps anyway. We picked $K = 10$ as a compromise.

The PPO agent starts at episode reward $-0.148$ (worse than the unmanaged baseline, which is what we expect from a randomly initialized policy) and converges to mean $+1.064$ with best $+1.536$ (Fig.~\ref{fig:ppo}). The reward is positive in absolute terms, which means the learned policy beats the unmanaged baseline on the composite metric $0.5 \Delta_{\text{aging}} + 0.3 \cdot b + 0.2 \cdot \Delta_{\text{lat}}$. The gap between mean reward and best reward is itself informative---if all episodes converged to roughly the same reward, the agent would be policy-collapsed. The spread we see suggests the agent has learned to specialize its strategy by workload context, which is the desired behaviour.

Fig.~\ref{fig:ppo_baselines} compares against two baselines across 3 random seeds. The unmanaged baseline is flat near zero, as expected. The greedy hotspot-migration policy (move the heaviest layer from the hottest cluster every step) reaches around $+0.6$ and then plateaus. PPO climbs past both. The shaded bands also show that the seed-to-seed variation is non-trivial---a single-seed PPO result would have been an unfair claim, which is why we report the multi-seed mean and standard deviation.

We are upfront that 5,120 timesteps is short by RL standards. The agent has clearly not saturated. We see this as an indication that more training would help rather than as a converged result.

\subsection{End-to-end picture}

When the pieces are stacked---NSGA-II for the offline mapping, PPO for runtime adjustment---the average peak-aging reduction is around $61.5\%$ from NSGA-II alone, with PPO providing an additional balancing effect at runtime. The trajectory forecast is what lets the PPO controller act before a hotspot fully forms rather than after. The framework is not a replacement for thermal management or DVFS; it sits above them as a workload-distribution layer.

\section{Limitations and Threats to Validity}
\label{sec:limitations}

We list these openly because we think a research paper that hides them is harder to trust than one that names them.

\textbf{Synthetic data.} All 40,000 training samples come from our own analytical simulator. There is no validation against silicon measurements or against an external benchmark. The reported $R^2 = 0.9976$ should therefore be read as ``the model fits the simulator's outputs very well''---which is a meaningful but limited claim. Real hardware has process variation, thermal noise, measurement noise, and aging trajectories that almost certainly do not factor as cleanly as our $0.4/0.4/0.2$ NBTI/HCI/TDDB weighting suggests. Calibrating the physics-model coefficients ($A$, $B$, $k$, $\beta$) against published silicon data, and then re-evaluating, is the most important next step.

\textbf{Short PPO training.} 5,120 timesteps is well short of the $10^5$--$10^6$ used in most published PPO results for hardware management. The reward curve in Fig.~\ref{fig:ppo} has not flattened. We suspect a longer run would give higher mean reward, possibly with a different action-distribution profile. We chose the short budget for reproducibility on commodity CPUs; treat the PPO numbers as ``learning is happening'' rather than ``this is the converged policy''.

\textbf{Small NSGA-II population.} $N_{\text{pop}} = 20$ for a $16^{15} \approx 10^{18}$ search space is small. The 91 Pareto solutions across five workloads are real and reproducible (fixed seed), but they are not exhaustive. Running with $N_{\text{pop}} = 100$ and $g_{\max} = 50$ would likely surface a denser front.

\textbf{Reward weights chosen by inspection.} The $(0.5, 0.3, 0.2)$ coefficients in Eq.~\ref{eq:reward} were selected after a few short pilot runs. We did not do a Pareto-style sweep over reward terms. A different weighting could shift the agent toward more aggressive aging reduction at the cost of latency, or vice versa.

\textbf{Temperature is a proxy, not a simulation.} The $x_4$ feature is an activity-and-energy-derived estimate, not the output of a thermal RC network. For a deployed system, replacing this with a per-node thermal simulator (or, ideally, on-die temperature sensors) would tighten the link between predicted and actual aging.

\textbf{Cross-workload generalization is untested.} We trained and evaluated on samples drawn from all five workloads. We did not train on, say, only ResNet-50 snapshots and test on ViT-B/16. That experiment would tell us whether the predictor is learning aging dynamics or memorizing per-workload patterns.

\textbf{Hyperparameter sensitivity for aging coefficients.} We did not perform a full sensitivity sweep over $A$, $B$, $k$, $\beta$. Rough manual perturbations (varying $A$ by $\pm 50\%$) did not change the qualitative pattern of results, but a proper sensitivity table is missing.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We set out to build a single framework that does three things together: predicts per-component aging on a DNN accelerator, forecasts where that aging is heading, and uses both signals to make scheduling decisions. The Hybrid GNN--Transformer reaches $R^2 = 0.9976$ on component-level prediction and the trajectory head produces a useful $R^2 \approx 0.866$ over a 10-step horizon. NSGA-II turns those predictions into 91 Pareto-optimal mappings across five workloads, with peak-aging reductions of $49$--$70\%$ depending on workload structure. A PPO controller, given the same predictions, learns a runtime policy that beats both an unmanaged baseline and a greedy hotspot-migration heuristic.

What remains uncertain---and we want to be honest about this---is how much of the predictor's accuracy survives contact with real silicon. The dataset is synthetic. The aging coefficients are taken from the literature without per-process calibration. The PPO budget is short. None of these break the framework, but each is a knob that real-world deployment would need to turn. We see three immediate priorities. First, validate the physics models against silicon measurements from at least one process node, even a coarse calibration would meaningfully sharpen the claims. Second, retrain PPO with $10^5$ timesteps on a GPU and re-report the numbers; we suspect the mean reward would climb noticeably, but cannot prove it from current runs. Third, replace the activity-derived temperature proxy with a real thermal simulator coupled into the data-generation loop.

The full code, training scripts, and figure-generation utilities are public.\footnote{\url{https://github.com/grizzleyyybear/adaptive-aging-aware-DNN}}
